Stock Market Glossary

Bear Market
A market characterized by falling stock prices. A bear market occurs when a broad market index falls by 20% or more from its most recent high.
Bull Market
A market characterized by rising stock prices. Often defined when key indices rise by 20% or more from their most recent lows.
Dividend
A payment made by a corporation to its shareholders, usually in the form of cash or additional stocks.
Initial Public Offering (IPO)
The first sale of stock by a company to the public. IPOs are often issued by younger, smaller companies seeking capital to expand.
Market Capitalization
The total value of all of a company's outstanding shares of stock. It is calculated by multiplying a company's share price by its number of outstanding shares.
P/E Ratio
Price-to-Earnings Ratio. A valuation ratio calculated by dividing the current share price by its earnings per share (EPS).
Portfolio
A collection of investments owned by an individual, institution, or mutual fund. These investments can include stocks, bonds, and other securities.
Short Selling
The practice of selling a security that isn't owned by the seller, but is promised to be delivered. It's a technique used to capitalize on an expected decline in the security's price.
Volume
The number of shares or contracts traded for a security or an entire exchange on a given day.
Yield
The income generated from an investment, expressed as an annual percentage of the investment's cost or current market value. For stocks, it usually refers to the dividend yield.
Blue-Chip Stocks
Shares of large, well-established, and financially stable companies with a history of reliable performance. These stocks are typically leaders in their industries.
Broker
A person or firm that is licensed to buy and sell securities on behalf of clients. Brokers can also provide research and financial planning services.
Day Trading
The buying and selling of securities within a single trading day, aiming to profit from short-term price movements.
Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)
A type of security that involves a collection of securities—such as stocks—that often tracks an underlying index. Unlike mutual funds, ETFs are listed on exchanges and trade throughout the day like an ordinary stock.
Liquidity
The ease with which an asset or security can be quickly bought or sold in the market without affecting its price. Highly liquid assets can be sold easily, while illiquid assets might require more time or might need to be sold at a discount.
Mutual Fund
A type of investment vehicle consisting of a portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities. Mutual funds give small or individual investors access to diversified, professionally managed portfolios at a low price.
Options
Financial derivatives that give buyers the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset (like stocks) at a predetermined price within a specified timeframe. Sellers of options have the obligation to fulfill the contract if the buyer decides to exercise the option.
Rally
A rapid increase in the general price level of the market or of the price of a stock. It's driven by increased buying, often due to positive news or sentiment.
Stock Split
An action by a company to divide its existing shares into multiple shares. Although the number of shares outstanding increases, the total value of the shares remains the same, which reduces the price per share.
Technical Analysis
A method of evaluating securities by analyzing statistics generated by market activity, such as past prices and volume. Technical analysts use charts and other tools to identify price patterns that can suggest future activity.
